# No More Mondays Team Voice Baseline

This file is the team voice baseline for No More Mondays. It contains 10 real student-facing messages written by Coach Albert. The Student Reply and Student Feedback skills use these as their primary voice reference when drafting in the team's voice.

**Who should upload this file:** every team member, in the first week. Drag this file into Project Knowledge alongside your Role Project. The Student Reply and Student Feedback skills will read it automatically.

**When you should add your own voice on top:** once you have been using Claude OS for a week or two and notice the drafts feel "too Albert" for your style. At that point you can add your personal voice samples in a separate file (`voice-samples-[your-name].md`) and upload it to Project Knowledge alongside this team file. The skills are designed to prefer your personal file when present, while still using this team file as a sanity check.

**Voice characteristics extracted from these messages:**
- Direct, caring, firm. Warm enough that people feel backed. Direct enough that they cannot miss the point.
- Grounded in specifics: numbers, examples, named patterns, real stakes.
- Formality level: 6 to 7 out of 10. Professional but conversational. Contractions used freely.
- Phrases used often: "bias toward movement", "let the math work for you", "I got you", "trust the process", "levers to pull", "singular focus"
- Phrases never used: em dashes (ever), "cinematic", "UGC" as jargon in pitches, corporate hedges, generic AI openers
- Emoji usage: none.
- Typical structure for long feedback: numbered sections, each with problem then why then fix then example, close with encouragement.

Message 2: 

What These Pitches Are Getting Wrong
There are a few core problems showing up across all four templates. Once you fix these at the structural level, every pitch you write will get more responses.

1. The Subject Lines Are Too Loud
"UGC Concepts to Drive More Conversions" reads like a marketing email, because it is one. The person receiving it knows immediately that you want something from them, and they skip it.
The best-performing subject lines are short, simple, and sound like a human wrote them. Something like:

"Content idea for [Brand Name]"
"Quick idea for [Brand Name]"
"Had an idea for you"

That is it. No jargon. No UGC. No "opportunity." Just a plain sentence that makes someone curious enough to open it.
Also, avoid the word "UGC" in your subject lines, and use it sparingly in the body too. The brands you are pitching are not always thinking in those terms. They care about results: more app installs, more bookings, more sales. Lead with outcomes, not industry labels.

2. You Are Opening Every Email Talking About Yourself
This is the most important thing to understand about cold outreach:
Nobody cares who you are until they care about what you can do for them.
Every single one of your templates jumps straight into "My name is Monica, I specialize in..." within the first few lines. That is the fastest way to lose someone's attention. They are not reading your pitch to learn about you. They are reading it to find out if it is relevant to them.
The fix is to flip the structure. Open with the idea. Open with something that makes them think "oh, that could actually help us." Create a loop in their brain that they want to close by reading further.
Instead of: "My name is Monica, I specialize in performance-driven content for apps and SaaS brands..."
Try something like: "I had an idea for a short video concept that I think could bring in more [app installs / bookings / sign-ups] for [Brand Name], specifically around [one specific angle relevant to their product]."
Now they are thinking about their problem, not about who you are. You have created a reason for them to keep reading. Only after you have given them that context and made the idea feel relevant should you briefly mention who you are and why you are the right person to execute it.

3. The Closing Questions Are Passive and Long-Winded
"Would you be open to reviewing my portfolio or exploring a few content directions?" is two questions in one, and neither of them creates urgency or moves the conversation forward.
"Would you be open to seeing a few tailored content ideas for your current or upcoming campaigns?" is a full sentence of corporate-speak.
The whole goal of your first pitch is one thing: get a reply.
You are not trying to close a deal in the first email. You are just trying to start a conversation. Your closing question needs to be short, direct, and easy to answer yes or no.
Something like:

"Would you be open to a quick chat so I can walk you through the specific ideas I had?"
"Want me to send over the concept in more detail?"
"Happy to send a couple of examples. Would that be useful?"

Short. Simple. One question. Easy to say yes to.

4. Drop the Word "Cinematic"
Never use the word "cinematic" in a pitch. It sounds like you are describing a film school project, and it does not mean anything useful to a brand that wants content that converts. Describe what the content does for them, not how it looks to you.

The Structure That Works
Every pitch should follow this flow:

Subject line: short, human, curiosity-driven
Opening: lead with the idea, not with yourself. Open a loop.
Middle: explain why the idea is relevant to their specific brand and what outcome it drives
Brief intro: now you mention who you are and why you are credible for this
Close: one short yes-or-no question that invites a reply

Everything else is noise.

Rewrite your four templates using this structure and bring them back. The bones of your ideas are solid. The sequencing and the framing just need to be rebuilt.
